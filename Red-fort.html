History of Red Fort

Commissioned by Shah Jahan in 1638 when he shifted his capital from Agra to Delhi, the Red Fort was designed by Ustad Ahmad Lahori, the same architect of the Taj Mahal. Constructed between 1638-1648, it served as the political and cultural center of the Mughal Empire. After the 1857 Revolt, the British captured it, and it remained under British rule until India's independence in 1947.

Architecture of Red Fort

Built using red sandstone, the fort showcases a blend of Persian, Timurid, and Indian architectural styles. Enclosed by massive 75-foot-high walls stretching over 2.4 km, it houses royal palaces, gardens, mosques, and audience halls.

Key Attractions Inside Red Fort

  • Lahori Gate - The main entrance of the fort, facing Chandni Chowk.
  • Diwan-i-Aam (Hall of Public Audience) - A large hall where the emperor addressed the public.
  • Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience) - A luxurious hall where the emperor met important guests.
  • Rang Mahal (Palace of Colors) - Used by the Mughal queens, decorated with mirrors and water channels.
  • Moti Masjid (Pearl Mosque) - A small white marble mosque built by Aurangzeb.
  • Mumtaz Mahal and Khas Mahal - Part of the royal quarters and the emperor‚Äôs private residence.
  • Hayat Bakhsh Bagh (Life-Giving Garden) - A large Mughal-style garden featuring fountains and pavilions.

Best Time to Visit Red Fort

October to March offers pleasant weather for exploring the fort. Independence Day (August 15th) is a special occasion when the fort is the center of national celebrations.

How to Reach Red Fort

By Metro: Nearest Metro Station is Chandni Chowk (Yellow Line), within walking distance.

By Road: Well-connected by buses, taxis, and auto-rickshaws.

By Train: Nearest railway stations are Old Delhi Railway Station (1.5 km) and New Delhi Railway Station (4 km).
